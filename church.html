Church History

Tracing the roots of the Christian faith from the Cross to the modern day.

1st Century

The Apostolic Age

Following the resurrection and ascension of Jesus Christ, the Holy Spirit descended at Pentecost. The Apostles, led by Peter and later Paul, began spreading the Gospel beyond Jerusalem to the Gentile world, establishing the very first churches.

2nd - 3rd Century

The Age of Martyrs

The early church faced fierce persecution under the Roman Empire. Despite the threat of death in arenas, the faith grew rapidly. Early Church Fathers and Apologists arose to defend the true Gospel against heresies.

4th Century

The Imperial Church

Emperor Constantine legalized Christianity in 313 AD. The Council of Nicaea (325 AD) was convened to formalize the doctrine of the Holy Trinity, giving us the Nicene Creed which defends the divinity of Jesus Christ.

11th Century

The Great Schism

Theological and political differences caused the first major split in Christian history in 1054 AD, dividing the church into the Western (Roman Catholic) and Eastern (Orthodox) branches.

16th Century

The Protestant Reformation

Sparked by figures like Martin Luther and John Calvin, the Reformation called the church back to scriptural authority. It emphasized that salvation is by grace alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone.

Present Day

The Global Church

Today, the body of Christ spans across every nation and tongue. The mission remains the same: to preach the Gospel of Jesus Christ to the ends of the earth and make disciples of all nations.

True Church of Christ

The Eastern Orthodox Church

Apostolic Foundations

The Eastern Orthodox Church traces its roots directly back to Jesus Christ and the Apostles. It was born out of the earliest Christian communities established in the 1st century, particularly in ancient centers like Jerusalem, Antioch, Alexandria, and Constantinople.

The Church maintains that it has preserved the original, unaltered teachings of the Apostles through an unbroken line of "Apostolic Succession." Historically, the Apostle Andrew is deeply venerated as the founder of the See of Constantinople, which remains the spiritual center of Orthodoxy today.

Source: Greek Orthodox Archdiocese of America

Mysticism and The Holy Trinity

Orthodox theology places a massive emphasis on the Holy Trinity and the mystery of the Incarnation. They believe deeply in Theosis—the lifelong process of becoming increasingly united with God through grace.

Worship is highly liturgical, engaging all the senses with incense, chanting, and sacred art. Icons (holy paintings of Christ and the saints) are venerated not as idols, but as "windows into heaven," helping believers connect visually and spiritually with the divine reality.

Source: Orthodox Church in America (OCA)

A Global Expansion

From the 1st century to the 11th century, the Eastern and Western churches were united. Following the Great Schism of 1054, the Orthodox Church maintained its distinct Eastern identity.

Missionaries like Cyril and Methodius spread the faith from the Mediterranean deep into Eastern Europe and the Slavic nations, inventing the Cyrillic alphabet to translate the Bible. Today, despite surviving centuries of Ottoman rule and Soviet persecution, the Orthodox Church has spread across every continent, with over 220 million members globally.

Source: OrthodoxWiki History

Guardians & Martyrs of Holy Tradition

The Eastern Orthodox Church places profound importance on the Early Church Fathers and Martyrs who defended the faith against early heresies with their theology and their blood.

St. Ignatius of Antioch (Martyr)

A disciple of the Apostle John, Ignatius was the third bishop of Antioch. When sentenced to death in Rome (c. 108 AD), he wrote seven monumental epistles defining early church structure. He passionately embraced his martyrdom, famously writing, "I am the wheat of God, and let me be ground by the teeth of the wild beasts, that I may be found the pure bread of Christ."

St. John Chrysostom

Known as the "Golden-Mouthed" for his incredibly eloquent preaching, he was the Archbishop of Constantinople. He lived a strict ascetic lifestyle, giving his wealth to the poor, and fiercely condemned the corruption of the wealthy elite and the royal court, which ultimately led to his tragic exile and death. His Divine Liturgy is still celebrated in the Orthodox Church every Sunday.

St. George the Great Martyr

A high-ranking soldier in the Roman army under Emperor Diocletian. When the Emperor ordered the systematic persecution of Christians, George boldly declared his faith. Despite enduring horrific tortures, he refused to sacrifice to pagan idols and was beheaded in 303 AD. His unwavering faith led to the conversion of many, including the Empress Alexandra.

Source: OCA - The Fathers of the Church

The Orthodox Canon & Deuterocanonicals

The Orthodox Church uses the oldest translation of the Old Testament, the Greek Septuagint. Because of this, the Orthodox Bible contains up to 76 or 78 books. It includes several historical and wisdom books (often called Apocrypha by Protestants) that were highly valued by the early Christians:

  • Tobit: A profound story of faith, angelology (featuring the Archangel Raphael), and divine providence.
  • Judith: A gripping historical tale of a faithful Jewish widow saving her people from an invading Assyrian army.
  • 1 and 2 Maccabees: Historical accounts of the Jewish revolt against the Seleucid empire, detailing the origin of Hanukkah and early beliefs in praying for the departed.
  • Wisdom of Solomon: A beautiful book of wisdom literature pointing toward the coming of the righteous suffering servant.
  • Sirach (Ecclesiasticus): A comprehensive collection of ethical teachings and practical wisdom for daily living.
  • Baruch (incl. Letter of Jeremiah): Prophetic writings dealing with the theology of exile and repentance.
  • 1 Esdras: A historical book related to the events of Ezra and Nehemiah.
  • Prayer of Manasseh: A deeply moving penitential prayer of repentance.
  • Psalm 151: An additional short psalm attributed to David after he defeated Goliath.
  • 3 Maccabees: A story set before the events of 1 and 2 Maccabees concerning the Jews in Egypt.
  • Additions to Esther and Daniel: Contains the stories of Susanna, Bel and the Dragon, and the Song of the Three Holy Children.
Source: OCA - Canon of Scripture

The Roman Catholic Church

Built on the Rock

The Roman Catholic Church is the largest Christian church in the world. It traces its history directly to Jesus Christ and the Twelve Apostles, particularly placing emphasis on Jesus' words to Peter: "You are Peter, and on this rock I will build my Church." (Matthew 16:18).

Following the Apostolic era, the center of the Western Church naturally gravitated toward Rome, where both St. Peter and St. Paul were martyred. The Catholic Church views itself as the one, holy, catholic (meaning "universal"), and apostolic church founded by Christ.

Source: USCCB - What We Believe

Faith, Reason, and Grace

Catholic theology emphasizes the harmonious relationship between faith and reason. At its core is the belief in the Holy Trinity, the Incarnation, and the Resurrection. Salvation is viewed as a gift of grace from God, which is received through faith and lived out through acts of love and charity.

The Church strongly believes in the "Communion of Saints"—the spiritual solidarity connecting the faithful on earth, the souls in Purgatory being purified, and the saints in Heaven. Mary, the Mother of God, holds a place of supreme honor as the greatest of the saints and the spiritual mother of all Christians.

Source: The Catechism of the Catholic Church

The Magisterium and The Papacy

A unique defining feature of Catholicism is its hierarchical structure of authority. The Church is led by the Pope, who is the Bishop of Rome and the Vicar of Christ on Earth, standing in the unbroken line of Apostolic Succession from St. Peter.

The Pope, along with the bishops in communion with him, form the Magisterium (the teaching authority of the Church). Catholics believe that the Holy Spirit guides the Magisterium to interpret Scripture and Tradition accurately, protecting the Church from officially teaching theological error regarding faith and morals.

Source: Catholic Answers - Papal Authority

Through Empires and Ages

The Catholic Church has been a central driving force in Western civilization. During the fall of the Roman Empire, it was the Church that preserved literacy, built the first hospitals, and established the university system across Europe.

After the Great Schism (1054) and the Protestant Reformation (16th Century), the Church underwent its own massive renewal during the Counter-Reformation (Council of Trent). Today, following the modernizations of the Second Vatican Council (1962–1965), the Catholic Church serves over 1.3 billion baptized members across the globe.

Source: The Holy See (Vatican)

The Catholic Canon (73 Books)

The Catholic Bible consists of 73 books (46 in the Old Testament, 27 in the New Testament). In the 4th century, St. Jerome translated the Scriptures into Latin (The Vulgate), which became the standard Bible for Western Christianity for over a thousand years.

While Protestant Bibles typically follow the 66-book canon established during the Reformation, the Catholic Church preserved the Deuterocanonical books. These books were part of the Septuagint (the Greek Old Testament) used by the early Church and provide a vital spiritual bridge between the prophets and the coming of Christ:

  • Tobit: A heartwarming story of faith, family, and divine providence, featuring the guidance of the Archangel Raphael. It emphasizes the importance of prayer, fasting, and almsgiving.
  • Judith: A powerful narrative of a heroic Jewish widow who, through her unwavering trust in God and great courage, saves her nation from the invading Assyrian army.
  • 1 and 2 Maccabees: These historical books detail the Jewish struggle for religious freedom against the Seleucid Empire. They explain the origin of Hanukkah and contain early evidence of Jewish belief in the resurrection and the efficacy of prayer for the departed.
  • Wisdom (of Solomon): A profound work of wisdom literature that speaks of the immortality of the soul and righteousness. It contains striking passages that many Christians see as direct foreshadowing of the suffering of Jesus.
  • Sirach (Ecclesiasticus): A comprehensive collection of ethical teachings and practical advice for daily life, teaching that "the fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom."
  • Baruch: Written by the secretary of the Prophet Jeremiah, this book includes a moving prayer of repentance for the exiles in Babylon and a poem praising Divine Wisdom.
  • Additions to Esther and Daniel: These sections include the Prayer of Azariah, the Song of the Three Holy Children, and the stories of Susanna and Bel and the Dragon, which are not found in the Hebrew versions of these books.
Source: USCCB - Understanding the Bible

The Seven Holy Sacraments

Sacraments are defined as "visible signs of invisible grace, instituted by Christ." They are the primary way Catholics receive God's grace throughout their spiritual journey from birth to death.

  • Baptism: Cleanses Original Sin and initiates a person into the Christian family.
  • Confirmation: The sealing of the Holy Spirit, strengthening the believer's faith.
  • The Eucharist (Holy Communion): The "source and summit" of the faith. Catholics believe the bread and wine literally become the Body, Blood, Soul, and Divinity of Jesus Christ (Transubstantiation).
  • Penance (Reconciliation/Confession): The forgiveness of sins committed after Baptism through a priest.
  • Anointing of the Sick: Spiritual (and sometimes physical) healing and comfort for the seriously ill.
  • Holy Orders: The ordination of men as bishops, priests, and deacons to serve the Church.
  • Matrimony: The sacred, lifelong covenant of marriage between a man and a woman.
Source: USCCB - The Seven Sacraments

Protestant Churches

Tracing the denominations born from the Great Reformation and subsequent Revivals.

The Methodist Church

The Holy Club of Oxford

The Methodist movement did not begin as a separate church, but as a revival movement within the Church of England in the 18th century. It was founded by two brothers, John and Charles Wesley, along with George Whitefield.

While studying at Oxford University, they formed a group dedicated to intense Bible study, fasting, and visiting the sick and imprisoned. Because of their incredibly methodical, strict, and disciplined approach to the Christian life, fellow students mockingly called them "Methodists"—a title they eventually embraced.

Source: UMC - History of Methodism

Grace and Christian Perfection

Methodist theology is deeply rooted in Arminianism, which emphasizes that God's grace is freely available to all people, not just a predestined few. A core belief is "Prevenient Grace"—the grace that surrounds us even before we are aware of God, drawing us toward Him.

John Wesley famously taught the doctrine of "Christian Perfection" or "Entire Sanctification." This does not mean believers are physically flawless, but rather that a Christian's heart can be so completely filled with the love of God and neighbor that the intentional desire to sin is removed.

Source: UMC - What We Believe

The Circuit Riders and Global Spread

When the Anglican church closed its doors to John Wesley’s intense preaching, he took to the fields, preaching to thousands of working-class coal miners and laborers. The movement exploded across Britain.

In the late 1700s, Methodism spread to America through figures like Francis Asbury and Thomas Coke. Because the American frontier was vast and lacking churches, Methodism utilized "Circuit Riders"—preachers who rode on horseback from town to town, enduring harsh weather and poverty to preach the Gospel.

Source: World Methodist Council

The 66 Books & The Wesleyan Quadrilateral

The Methodist Church holds to the 66-book canon (39 Old Testament books and 27 New Testament books), excluding the Apocrypha found in Catholic and Orthodox Bibles.

John Wesley’s approach to scripture is uniquely summarized by scholars as the Wesleyan Quadrilateral. This teaches that theological truth is revealed through four lenses:

  • Scripture: The primary, foundational, and absolute source of Christian truth.
  • Tradition: The history and teachings of the early church.
  • Reason: God gave us a mind to logically process and comprehend His Word.
  • Experience: The personal, inward testimony of the Holy Spirit.
Source: UMC - Our Roots in the Bible

The Baptist Church

The River of Repentance

The Baptist tradition takes its primary theological inspiration from John the Baptist. Historically, the modern Baptist denomination emerged in the early 17th century among English Separatists.

Led by figures like John Smyth and Thomas Helwys in 1609, these early believers determined that a true church should only consist of people who have consciously chosen to follow Christ. They rejected infant baptism, establishing the core Baptist practice of "Credobaptism" (Believer's Baptism) by full immersion in water.

Source: Baptist World Alliance

Soul Competency & Autonomy

Baptists are fiercely independent in their theology. They believe in Soul Competency—the idea that every individual is fully capable of reading the Bible, communicating directly with God, and making spiritual decisions without needing a priest as a mediator.

Furthermore, they strictly hold to the Autonomy of the Local Church. Unlike Catholics or Methodists who have a top-down hierarchy, every single Baptist church is independent, governing itself under the authority of Christ alone.

Source: SBC - The Baptist Faith & Message

Religious Liberty and Expansion

Early Baptists faced severe persecution in England and the early American colonies for refusing to conform to state religions. Roger Williams, a Baptist pioneer, founded Rhode Island in 1636 to guarantee absolute religious liberty.

Through the Great Awakenings, powerful preachers like Charles Spurgeon and evangelists like Billy Graham, the movement exploded. Today, it is one of the largest Protestant families in the world.

Source: Baptist History & Heritage Society

The Supreme Authority (66 Books)

For Baptists, the 66-book Protestant Bible is the absolute, final, and solitary authority for all matters of faith and practice (Sola Scriptura).

Because there is no central human authority, the Bible alone is the constitution of the church. Many Baptist traditions strongly emphasize the literal interpretation, inerrancy, and infallibility of Scripture. Every sermon and church rule is weighed directly against the written Word of God.

Source: SBC - On The Scriptures

The Lutheran Church

The Spark of the Reformation

The Lutheran Church represents the oldest branch of Protestantism, tracing its origins directly to Martin Luther, a German monk and theology professor. In 1517, Luther famously nailed his 95 Theses to the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg.

He was protesting the Catholic Church's sale of "indulgences" (certificates reducing punishment for sins). Luther did not intend to start a new church; he simply wanted to reform the existing one. However, his writings sparked the Protestant Reformation, fundamentally altering the course of world history.

Source: LCMS - History

The Three Solas

Lutheran theology is famously summarized by three Latin phrases known as the "Solas":

  • Sola Gratia (Grace Alone): God loves and saves us strictly out of His sheer grace, not because of anything we do to earn it.
  • Sola Fide (Faith Alone): Justification comes exclusively through faith in Christ’s sacrifice, completely apart from human works.
  • Sola Scriptura (Scripture Alone): The Bible is the only infallible source of divine revelation.

Unlike Baptists or Methodists, Lutherans hold a very high view of the sacraments, believing that Christ is truly and physically present "in, with, and under" the bread and wine of Holy Communion (often called Sacramental Union).

Source: LCMS - Beliefs

The Diet of Worms to the World

After refusing to recant his writings at the Diet of Worms in 1521, Luther was excommunicated and forced into hiding. During his exile, he translated the New Testament into everyday German, putting the Word of God directly into the hands of common people for the first time.

The movement spread rapidly through Germany and Scandinavia, largely adopted by state governments. Today, the Lutheran tradition is a global communion of over 77 million Christians, anchored by historical documents like the Augsburg Confession.

Source: Lutheran World Federation

Law and Gospel

The Lutheran Church adheres to the 66-book Protestant canon. While Luther himself famously debated the placement of certain books (like James and Revelation), the church maintains a steadfast commitment to the absolute authority of Scripture.

A defining characteristic of how Lutherans read the Bible is the strict distinction between Law and Gospel. The "Law" (God's commands) shows us our sin and our desperate need for a Savior, while the "Gospel" (God's promises) shows us our Savior and the free gift of forgiveness in Christ.

Source: LCMS - On The Bible

The Anglican Church

The English Reformation & Via Media

The Anglican Church (Church of England) originated in the 16th century. While theological reform was already brewing in England, the official split from Rome was triggered by King Henry VIII’s desire to annul his marriage to Catherine of Aragon.

Under Queen Elizabeth I, the church established the Via Media (The Middle Way). It retained much of the Catholic liturgical style, sacraments, and the historic structure of bishops and priests, while fully embracing Protestant theology (like justification by faith). The Archbishop of Canterbury serves as the spiritual head, acting as the "first among equals."

Source: The Anglican Communion - History

The Book of Common Prayer

Instead of a single massive doctrinal confession, Anglican beliefs are uniquely unified by their worship. The Book of Common Prayer, compiled largely by Thomas Cranmer in 1549, is the beautiful, poetic heart of Anglicanism, guiding their daily prayers and Sunday liturgies.

Theological boundaries are broadly defined by the Thirty-Nine Articles of Religion. Anglicans believe in two primary sacraments instituted directly by Christ: Baptism and the Eucharist (Holy Communion), though they also practice confirmation, ordination, marriage, absolution, and anointing of the sick as sacramental rites.

Source: Church of England - What We Believe

From England to the Ends of the Earth

As the British Empire expanded across the globe in the 17th to 19th centuries, it brought the Church of England with it. This planted Anglican communities in North America, Africa, Australia, and Asia.

Following the American Revolution, American Anglicans had to separate from the English Crown, forming the Episcopal Church. Today, the global Anglican Communion is the third-largest Christian communion in the world, with over 85 million members, experiencing its most explosive growth in countries like Nigeria and Uganda.

Source: Anglican Member Churches

The 66 Books & The Apocrypha

The Anglican Church holds that the 66 books of the Old and New Testaments "contain all things necessary to salvation" (Article VI of the Thirty-Nine Articles).

However, the Anglican position on the Deuterocanonical books (the Apocrypha) is unique among Protestants. While they do not use these books to establish core doctrine, they still include them in their lectionary and read them in church for "example of life and instruction of manners." Because of this, many Anglican Bibles include the Apocrypha wedged between the Old and New Testaments.

Source: Church of England - Article VI on Scripture

The Presbyterian Church

The Reformed Tradition & Rule of Elders

The Presbyterian Church finds its roots in the 16th-century Protestant Reformation, primarily guided by the brilliant French theologian John Calvin in Geneva, Switzerland, and later powerfully brought to Scotland by John Knox.

The very name "Presbyterian" comes from the Greek word presbuteros, meaning "elder." Unlike the Catholic, Orthodox, or Anglican churches (which are governed by bishops in a top-down hierarchy), or the Baptists (who are purely independent congregationalists), Presbyterians are governed by a council of elected elders, balancing local authority with regional accountability.

Source: PC(USA) - Presbyterian History

The Sovereignty of God

Presbyterian theology is strictly Reformed (or Calvinist). The absolute centerpiece of their belief is the supreme Sovereignty of God—the idea that God is in complete and total control of the universe, history, and human salvation.

They strongly emphasize Covenant Theology, viewing the entire Bible as one unified story of God making and keeping His covenants with His people. They also practice infant baptism, not to wash away Original Sin (as in Catholicism), but as a covenant sign similar to circumcision in the Old Testament, bringing the child into the visible church community.

Source: PCA - What We Believe

From the Scottish Highlands to America

John Knox’s fiery preaching established the Church of Scotland (The Kirk) in 1560, making it the mother church of global Presbyterianism. In 1643, the English Parliament called the Westminster Assembly, which produced the Westminster Confession of Faith—the defining historical document of Presbyterian beliefs.

When Scottish and Scots-Irish immigrants moved to America in the 17th and 18th centuries, they brought their fiercely independent, elder-led faith with them. Presbyterians were so heavily involved in the American Revolution that British officials occasionally referred to it as the "Presbyterian Rebellion." Today, Reformed and Presbyterian churches represent tens of millions of believers globally.

Source: The Church of Scotland - Our History

The Word Preached and Studied

Presbyterians hold to the standard 66-book Protestant Bible as the inspired, infallible, and absolute Word of God. They place an incredibly high premium on biblical literacy, education, and deep theological study.

Worship services historically center around the pulpit rather than the altar, signifying that the exposition (detailed preaching) of the Word is the climax of the service. Pastors are historically called "Teaching Elders" who undergo rigorous academic and theological training in original biblical languages (Greek and Hebrew) to ensure they rightly divide the Word of Truth.

Source: Westminster Confession - Of the Holy Scripture

The Pentecostal Church

The Azusa Street Revival

While the Holy Spirit has been active since the Early Church, the modern Pentecostal denomination traces its immediate roots to the early 20th century. It began with fervent prayer meetings in 1901 under Charles Parham in Kansas, and exploded globally during the Azusa Street Revival (1906–1909) in Los Angeles, led by an African-American preacher named William J. Seymour.

The movement was named after the biblical Day of Pentecost (Acts 2), where the early disciples were filled with the Holy Spirit and spoke in other tongues. Early Pentecostals believed they were experiencing a modern-day restoration of that exact same apostolic power.

Source: Assemblies of God - History

Baptism in the Holy Spirit & Fire

Pentecostal theology is distinct because of its emphasis on the "Baptism in the Holy Spirit," which they believe is an empowering experience distinct from and subsequent to salvation. The initial physical evidence of this baptism is traditionally considered to be speaking in tongues (glossolalia).

They are Continuationists, meaning they firmly believe that all the spiritual gifts mentioned in the New Testament—such as divine healing, prophecy, and the casting out of demons—are fully active and necessary for the Church today. Worship services are highly expressive, spontaneous, and emotionally engaged.

Source: AG - Fundamental Truths

A Global Outpouring

Despite being mocked by mainstream society and traditional denominations in its early days, Pentecostalism grew at an unprecedented rate. Its emphasis on personal spiritual encounters bypassed rigid intellectual barriers, allowing it to spread rapidly among the working class and marginalized communities.

In the 1960s, the "Charismatic Movement" saw Pentecostal theology spill over into traditional Catholic, Anglican, and mainline Protestant churches. Today, classical Pentecostals (like the Assemblies of God and Church of God in Christ) combined with the broader Charismatic movement represent over 600 million Christians worldwide—the second-largest Christian group after Catholicism.

Source: Pew Research - History of Pentecostalism

The Living, Active Word

Pentecostals hold to the traditional 66-book Protestant Bible, believing it to be the verbally inspired and infallible Word of God. It is the absolute rule for faith and conduct.

What makes the Pentecostal approach to Scripture unique is their experiential reading of it. They do not view the Book of Acts as merely a history book of the past, but as a living blueprint for the present. They expect the miracles, healings, and divine encounters written in the pages of the Bible to happen in their own lives and church services today.

Source: AG - The Scriptures Inspired
